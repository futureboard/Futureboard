# Futureboard Trademark and Third-Party Fork Policy

Effective: July 15, 2026

This policy explains how the Futureboard names and branding may be used with
copies, forks, modified versions, distributions, services, and other projects.
It is intended to protect users from confusion about who publishes, supports,
or endorses a product while preserving the freedoms granted for Community
Edition source code.

## 1. Software rights and trademark rights are separate

Source code identified as Futureboard Community Edition is available under the
[MIT License](LICENSE). The MIT License permits copying, modification, and
redistribution of that covered code, subject to its terms.

That copyright license does not, by itself, grant permission to use
Futureboard trademarks as the name, logo, or source identifier of a modified
product, service, company, or distribution. Trademark permissions are governed
by this policy and applicable law.

Futureboard Exclusive Edition source, activation services, private modules,
credentials, signing materials, and other material not included in the public
Community Edition repository are not licensed under the Community Edition MIT
License unless a separate written license expressly says otherwise.

## 3. Uses that do not require prior permission

Subject to applicable law, you may use the Futureboard word marks in plain
text — without Futureboard logos or trade dress — when reasonably necessary to:

- truthfully refer to the official Futureboard project or an unmodified
  official release;
- link to the official repository or website;
- state that independent software is compatible with Futureboard, if true;
- state that a project is based on or forked from Futureboard Community
  Edition, if true and accompanied by the fork notice in section 5; or
- preserve copyright, license, attribution, repository history, and factual
  provenance notices required or permitted by the MIT License.

Use only as much of a mark as is reasonably needed for the factual reference.
The presentation must not suggest that Futureboard publishes, certifies,
sponsors, supports, or endorses the third-party project.

A source-hosting platform may display the original repository name as part of
its ordinary fork relationship or history. This does not authorize a modified
binary, installer, package, service, or release to use Futureboard as its own
product branding.

## 4. Uses requiring prior written permission

Unless applicable law provides otherwise, prior written permission is required
to:

- distribute a modified product under a Futureboard product name or logo;
- use a Futureboard Mark in a company, organization, application, package,
  executable, service, domain, social-media account, event, or commercial
  offering name;
- use Futureboard logos, icons, banners, or trade dress as the primary branding
  of a third-party project;
- create merchandise, promotional material, or advertising featuring a
  Futureboard Mark other than for factual commentary;
- claim or imply official, certified, approved, partner, sponsor, or endorsed
  status; or
- use a name or visual identity confusingly similar to a Futureboard Mark.

Permission must be explicit and in writing. Silence, accepting a contribution,
linking to a fork, or failing to object to a prior use does not grant permission.

## 5. Requirements for forks using Futureboard Marks

If you use Futureboard Marks to describe the provenance of a modified Community
Edition distribution or publicly operated service, you must:

1. Choose a distinct product name, logo, application identity, and release
   branding that do not create confusion with Futureboard.
2. Remove Futureboard logos and primary Futureboard branding from modified
   binaries, installers, splash screens, store listings, update channels, and
   promotional material. Factual attribution may remain in documentation or an
   About/Credits view.
3. Preserve the MIT copyright and license notices for covered source code and
   comply with applicable third-party licenses and notices.
4. Clearly identify material modifications and direct users to the fork's own
   support, security-reporting, privacy, release, and update channels.
5. Do not represent that the fork includes, unlocks, or is licensed for
   Futureboard Exclusive Edition components unless you hold a separate written
   license that expressly permits that representation.
6. Display the following notice prominently in the fork's README and near any
   download or distribution page:

   > This project is an independent third-party fork of Futureboard Community
   > Edition. It is not affiliated with, endorsed by, sponsored by, or supported
   > by the Futureboard project or its maintainers.

You may add a factual sentence such as "Based on Futureboard Community Edition"
after your own product name, provided your own branding is more prominent and
the non-affiliation notice appears with comparable visibility.

## 6. Unmodified redistribution

You may truthfully identify an exact, unmodified copy of official Community
Edition source as Futureboard Community Edition. If you redistribute official
binaries or installers, you must clearly identify yourself as an independent
mirror or distributor, must not imply that your channel is official, and must
not alter the binaries, signatures, update configuration, or bundled notices.

Any modified build must follow section 5 instead.

## 7. Exclusive Edition and activation services

The public `exclusive` build-feature name and shared extension hooks do not grant
access to, a license for, or redistribution rights in Futureboard Exclusive
Edition. Private source under `crates/ExclusiveEdition`, license activation
infrastructure, service endpoints, signing keys, and related assets are governed
by separate terms supplied to authorized recipients.

You may not market a Community Edition fork as "Exclusive," "licensed,"
"activated," or otherwise associated with Futureboard's commercial edition in a
way likely to confuse users.
